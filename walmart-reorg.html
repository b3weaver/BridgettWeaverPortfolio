The situation

Three risks.
One leader.

Within my first two months at Walmart, a return-to-office mandate triggered significant attrition. Then a senior manager departed. Two weeks later, our director left. A new EVP joined, instituted a hiring freeze and began preparing a large-scale UX reorganization. Content design was operating at roughly 50% staffing. Contractors supplemented capacity. Work volume did not decrease.

I became the only remaining content design leader. The organization faced three compounding risks — and none of them could wait.

Team instability

  • Unclear on expectations
  • Uncertain about role security

Strategic ambiguity

  • Limited executive communication

"The immediate need was not more output. It was stability, clarity and trust."

My approach

Three tracks.
Run in parallel.

I ran three simultaneous workstreams: stabilizing the team, resetting the operating model and institutionalizing the practice. None of these could wait for the others.

Track 01 — Stabilize

I started by listening. Through 1:1 conversations and group sessions, I heard that the team felt undervalued, overextended and unsure about their place in the organization. I established recurring content design forums for open discussion and critique. I created biweekly team meetings to share everything I knew about the EVP's reorg plans — no filtering, no sugarcoating. I gave explicit permission to say no to misaligned or low-impact work, and reframed expectations from "support everything" to "prioritize high-impact involvement."

This created psychological safety during a period of sustained ambiguity. People can handle uncertainty. What they can't handle is uncertainty without acknowledgment.

Track 02 — Reset the operating model

The previous model positioned content design as reactive support. Designers would request help directly, and writers would absorb whatever came in — often 10 to 20 designers per writer with no prioritization. I shifted the team to a leader-aligned partnership model instead.

I aligned each content designer to a vertical leader rather than individual designers. I established priority alignment with product area leadership, reduced scattered intake to structured leader-aligned requests and clarified when content design should engage — and when it shouldn't. The goal was sustainable impact, not constant availability.

Track 03 — Institutionalize

I served as the central coordination point for content design across verticals — directing and triaging work, escalating systemic issues, building relationships with product and engineering and ensuring continuity while contractors supplemented capacity. At the same time, I stepped into a senior manager role within one vertical while maintaining cohesion across all teams.

To reduce ambiguity long-term, I initiated the creation of the Content Design Playbook.

Outcomes

The team held.
The discipline grew.

The broader reorganization resolved into a decentralized model aligned to vertical product areas. Content design entered that new structure with clarity, documented operating norms and a shared identity — because those things had been built during the chaos, not after it.

  • Team morale stabilized during 50% staffing and sustained leadership uncertainty
  • Burnout reduced through priority-based engagement and explicit permission to protect scope
  • Content designers gained visibility with vertical leadership instead of staying invisible support
  • Reactive intake model replaced with structured, leader-aligned partnership framework
  • Content Design Playbook established repeatable practice that scales beyond any individual
  • Team entered post-reorg vertical structure with cohesion, clarity and documented standards
